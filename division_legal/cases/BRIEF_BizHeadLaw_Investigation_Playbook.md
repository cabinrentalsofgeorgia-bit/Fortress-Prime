### What This Means for Defense

If BizHeadLaw ever does file suit, the ERC enforcement landscape creates multiple defense angles:

1. **Unclean Hands Doctrine:** If BizHeadLaw is found to be an abusive promoter, they cannot enforce a contract born from potentially improper practices. A firm under investigation (or matching the IRS profile of an "ERC mill") arguing in court that a client owes contingency fees opens itself to discovery about its own practices.

2. **Unconscionability:** A 20% contingency fee for 5 days of work (intake to filing) on a mass-production basis may be deemed unconscionable, particularly if the firm's eligibility analysis was superficial.

3. **Duress / Undue Influence:** BizHeadLaw's collection emails threaten to "notify the IRS that the refunds remain outstanding" and to "take legal action." The implicit threat of triggering an IRS review to pressure payment could constitute economic duress.

4. **Regulatory Exposure as Leverage:** In discovery, CROG could subpoena BizHeadLaw's client lists, filing practices, fee structures, and IRS correspondence. If BizHeadLaw's practices are questionable, they will not want this information made public. This makes them more likely to settle or drop the claim than to litigate.

5. **Statute of Limitations:** Georgia's statute of limitations on written contracts is 6 years (O.C.G.A. § 9-3-24). The agreement was signed March 29, 2023 — the clock started then. However, BizHeadLaw's theory is that the fee became due when refund checks were received, which they cannot prove without Gary's cooperation or IRS access.

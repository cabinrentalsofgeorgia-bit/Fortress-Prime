## V. DEFENSE ANALYSIS

### Scenario A: Gary Did NOT Receive the Refund Checks

**This is the strongest defense position.**

1. **Contingency Fee Not Triggered:** The agreement explicitly states fees "are only due after you receive your ERC check(s) from the IRS." If no checks were received, no fee is owed. Period.

2. **IRS Moratorium:** The IRS imposed a processing moratorium on ERC claims in September 2023. BizHeadLaw's own emails (Doc #14, Sandra Llera, June 2024) acknowledge: "Although the IRS is delaying on processing the refund checks due to the Moratorium they passed back in September 2023." Many April 2023 claims are still in the queue.

3. **BizHeadLaw Admits Uncertainty:** Every follow-up email asks Gary to "confirm whether you have received your ERC refund checks" — meaning they don't actually know. They are assuming receipt based on timing ("most of the other clients...already received at least 1 or 2 refund checks").

4. **TaxNow Push Proves They Don't Know:** If BizHeadLaw knew the refunds were issued, they wouldn't need Gary to register for TaxNow to track the claims. Their December 2024 and 2025 emails explicitly state they need TaxNow access to verify IRS records.

5. **Risk of IRS Complications:** Doc #26 threatens to "notify the IRS that the refunds for the above tax periods remain outstanding." If Gary didn't receive checks, this action could actually help — it would prompt IRS to investigate and reissue.

**Recommended Action (Scenario A):**
- Respond to BizHeadLaw stating refund checks were NOT received.
- Request they call IRS on Gary's behalf (which they offered in Doc #24).
- Sign and return Form 8821 to authorize them to check IRS records.
- This simultaneously defuses the collection threat and gets the claim status resolved.

### Scenario B: Gary DID Receive Some or All Refund Checks

**If checks were received and deposited, the contingency fee is owed per the agreement.**

1. **Obligation is Clear:** The signed agreement makes the fee contingent on receipt. If received, payment was due "within five days."

2. **Extended Non-Communication is a Problem:** 2+ years of silence creates legal exposure. BizHeadLaw can argue bad faith or breach of the Client Services Agreement.

3. **Fee Amount May Be Negotiable:** $43,400.28 on $217,001.42 is ~20%. Industry standard for ERC contingency firms ranges 15-25%. However, the actual work done (5 days from intake to IRS filing) may support a negotiation for reduced fees.

4. **Potential ERC Disallowance Defense:** If any quarters were disallowed by the IRS (which BizHeadLaw's own emails say is happening to many clients), the fee for those quarters would not be owed. BizHeadLaw wouldn't know about disallowances if Gary hasn't responded.

**Recommended Action (Scenario B):**
- Engage an independent attorney to review the Client Services Agreement.
- Determine exact amounts received per quarter.
- Negotiate a reduced lump-sum settlement (target 50-60% of claimed amount).
- Request detailed accounting: fee per quarter, any disallowances, reductions.

### Scenario C: Partial Receipt

If some quarters' refunds were received and others weren't, only fees on received amounts are owed. BizHeadLaw's demand appears to be for all 4 quarters without knowing which (if any) were received.
